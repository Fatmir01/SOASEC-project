# **Equal Time, Equal Opportunity**

## **Setting**

A mid-sized renewable energy engineering firm based in Valencia, specializing in solar infrastructure projects across Southern Europe. The company employs around 250 staff, including engineers, technicians, and administrative personnel, and has recently expanded into rural development initiatives.

## **Characters**

* **Lucía Ferrer** – Human resources director leading internal policy reforms
* **Mateo Ruiz** – Senior electrical engineer and team leader managing field operations
* **Noor Haddad** – Project analyst balancing early-career progression with family responsibilities
* **Sergio Vázquez** – Chief operations officer responsible for company-wide productivity and staffing

## **The Story**

The issue emerged during an internal workforce review initiated by Lucía Ferrer. Despite strong recruitment of women graduates, retention rates for women dropped sharply after three years. Promotion data revealed another disparity: only 18% of leadership roles were held by women, even though they represented nearly 40% of entry-level hires.

Lucía presented the findings to Sergio and Mateo. “We’re not losing talent randomly,” she explained. “We’re losing women at the point where work-life balance becomes critical—especially after starting families.”

Mateo, initially skeptical, responded, “Field operations are demanding. Deadlines, travel—it’s not always flexible.”

Noor, invited to share her experience, added candidly, “It’s not just about flexibility. It’s about predictability and support. When my childcare arrangement fell through, I had no option but to take unpaid leave. That stalled my career progression.”

Recognizing the structural nature of the issue, the leadership team committed to aligning their practices with the EU Gender Equality Strategy 2020-2025, particularly the implementation of the Work-Life Balance Directive.

The first measure was the introduction of a formal flexible working framework. Employees across all departments, including field engineers, were given access to adaptable schedules, remote planning days, and compressed workweeks. Crucially, the policy mandated equal eligibility for all genders, encouraging men to take parental leave and flexible arrangements without stigma.

Second, the company established a childcare support program. Partnering with local providers, they secured subsidized childcare slots for employees, including in nearby rural areas where project sites were located. Emergency childcare vouchers were also introduced for unexpected situations.

Third, Lucía implemented a “return-to-career pathway.” Employees returning from parental leave were offered a phased reintegration plan, including reduced workloads for the first three months, mentorship pairing, and guaranteed eligibility for promotion reviews within six months.

To address cultural barriers, Sergio launched an internal campaign titled “Shared Responsibility,” featuring male employees who had taken parental leave. Mateo himself participated, taking four weeks of leave after the birth of his second child. In a team meeting, he stated, “If we expect balance, we all have to model it. This isn’t just a policy—it’s a shift in how we work.”

The company also began tracking usage of flexible arrangements and parental leave by gender, publishing quarterly reports to ensure transparency and accountability.

Within one year, measurable improvements were evident. Retention rates for women after three years increased by 25%, and the proportion of men taking parental leave rose from 8% to 41%. Promotion rates for women improved significantly, with women now holding 30% of leadership roles. Employee satisfaction surveys showed a 35% increase in perceived work-life balance across all staff.

Reflecting on the transformation, Noor noted, “I didn’t have to choose between my career and my family. That changed everything.”

Lucía concluded in her annual report, “By implementing concrete measures under the EU Gender Equality Strategy 2020-2025, we’ve not only closed internal gaps but strengthened our entire workforce.”

The company’s approach was later cited as a regional best practice in aligning economic performance with gender-equal labor policies.
