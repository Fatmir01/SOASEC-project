# **Balancing the Scales of Opportunity**

## **Setting**
This legend takes place at **Alentejo GreenWorks**, a renewable‑energy cooperative located in **Évora, Portugal**. The cooperative manages solar‑energy installations across rural municipalities and employs technicians, analysts and community‑outreach specialists.  

## **Characters**
- **Marisa Duarte** — Operations Manager; oversees staffing and workforce planning across project sites.  
- **Tomasz Kurek** — Senior Solar Technician; advocates for fair training opportunities for all employees.  
- **Leila Ben Amar** — Financial Inclusion Officer; responsible for supporting entrepreneurship and access to funding.  
- **Hugo Ferreira** — Policy and Compliance Advisor; monitors alignment with EU labour‑market and equality frameworks.  
- **Rosa Jiménez** — Apprentice Technician; a young mother re‑entering the labour market after a career break.

## **The Story**
When Rosa joined Alentejo GreenWorks as an apprentice technician, she was excited to enter a sector traditionally dominated by men. Yet within weeks, Marisa noticed a pattern: Rosa was frequently absent from afternoon training sessions. When she asked why, Rosa hesitated before admitting that the only childcare available in her village closed at 14:00. “I want to stay in this job,” she said quietly, “but I can’t be in two places at once.”

Marisa brought the issue to the weekly management meeting. Hugo immediately recognised the broader structural challenge. “This is exactly what the **EU Gender Equality Strategy 2020‑2025** highlights,” he explained. “Women’s participation in the labour market is limited when work‑life balance isn’t supported. We need to align with the **Work‑Life‑Balance Directive** and go beyond the minimum standards.”

The team began by conducting an internal assessment of working patterns, pay levels and training access. They discovered that several women in administrative roles were also working reduced hours due to care responsibilities, limiting their chances for promotion. Meanwhile, men rarely requested flexible arrangements, even when eligible.

“We need to normalise flexibility for everyone,” Tomasz argued. “If only women use it, the imbalance continues.”

Together, they designed a multi‑layered plan:

- **Flexible scheduling** for all staff, including technicians, with equal encouragement for men and women to use it.  
- A **childcare partnership** with a local cooperative to extend service hours in rural areas, subsidised through regional cohesion‑policy funds.  
- A **transparent pay‑mapping exercise** to identify potential gender pay gaps, with results shared openly across the organisation.  
- A new **training‑rotation model** allowing employees with care responsibilities to complete technical modules remotely or during morning shifts.  
- A pilot programme led by Leila to support **women‑led green‑energy micro‑enterprises**, using InvestEU‑aligned funding streams.

During a team workshop, Marisa addressed the staff directly. “Equal participation isn’t just a principle,” she said. “It’s an economic necessity. We need every talent we have.”

The cultural shift began slowly but steadily. Hugo launched an internal campaign explaining how national tax and benefit systems can create **disincentives for second earners**, helping employees understand their rights and options. Tomasz volunteered to take afternoon training slots so that colleagues with care duties could rotate into morning sessions. Men began requesting flexible hours to share childcare, reducing the stigma around doing so.

Three months later, the cooperative conducted its first monitoring cycle aligned with the **European Semester** indicators on gender equality. The results were striking:  
- Women’s participation in technical training increased by **45%**.  
- The gender pay gap, previously hidden due to lack of transparency, was reduced by **12%** after adjustments.  
- Two women‑led solar‑maintenance micro‑enterprises were launched with Leila’s support.  
- Staff satisfaction with work‑life balance rose across all genders.

Rosa, now fully integrated into the technician team, completed her certification ahead of schedule. “I didn’t need special treatment,” she said during the annual assembly. “I just needed the same chance to thrive.”

Alentejo GreenWorks documented its reforms and shared them with regional authorities as a model for closing gender employment gaps in rural areas. The cooperative’s leadership reaffirmed that their progress—strengthening work‑life balance, improving pay transparency, and promoting women’s entrepreneurship—constituted concrete, measurable compliance with the **EU Gender Equality Strategy 2020‑2025**, helping build a truly gender‑equal economy.
