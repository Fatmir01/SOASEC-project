# **Weaving Equality Into Every Policy**

## **Setting**
This legend takes place at **Danube Futures Institute**, a cross‑disciplinary research centre in **Bratislava, Slovakia**, focused on climate adaptation, digital innovation and public‑health policy. The institute collaborates closely with EU agencies and national ministries to pilot inclusive policy approaches.

## **Characters**
- **Katarína Havelková** — Director of Strategic Policy Integration; leads cross‑sectoral initiatives.  
- **Milan Petrescu** — Climate Adaptation Specialist; analyses social impacts of environmental policies.  
- **Zahra Al‑Khatib** — Digital Inclusion Researcher; focuses on gender gaps in digital skills and access.  
- **Elodie Marchand** — Public Health Analyst; examines gender‑specific health risks.  
- **Sofia Dimitrova** — Community Liaison Officer; brings intersectional perspectives from migrant, Roma and disability communities.

## **The Story**
The Danube Futures Institute had long prided itself on evidence‑based policy research, but during a quarterly review, Katarína noticed a recurring flaw: each department produced strong analyses, yet none systematically integrated a **gender perspective**. “We’re missing the bigger picture,” she said during a staff meeting. “The **EU Gender Equality Strategy 2020‑2025** makes it clear—gender mainstreaming must be embedded in every policy area, especially in the green and digital transitions.”

To address this, she proposed creating an internal **Equality Integration Task Group**, mirroring the Commission’s **Task Force for Equality**. Milan, Zahra, Elodie and Sofia volunteered immediately.

Their first task was to review three major projects: a climate‑adaptation plan for rural municipalities, a digital‑skills programme for secondary schools and a health‑screening initiative for low‑income communities.

Milan began. “Our climate‑adaptation plan assumes equal mobility and access to resources,” he explained. “But women in rural areas rely more on public transport and face higher energy‑poverty risks. We need gender‑differentiated impact assessments.”

Zahra added, “And in our digital‑skills programme, we’re not addressing the fact that girls outperform boys in digital literacy but remain underrepresented in ICT pathways. We should align with the **Digital Education Action Plan** and the ‘Women in Digital’ commitments.”

Elodie highlighted gaps in the health initiative. “We’re not accounting for gender‑specific cancer risks or the findings expected from the **EU Beating Cancer Plan**. And substance‑abuse patterns differ for women and girls, which the **EU Drugs Agenda 2021–2025** emphasises.”

Sofia listened carefully before speaking. “And none of these programmes consider intersectionality. A Roma girl with a disability faces different barriers than a migrant woman or a single father. Our policies must reflect these layered realities.”

The team set to work, developing a comprehensive mainstreaming framework:

- **Mandatory gender‑impact assessments** for all institute projects, using indicators aligned with EIGE methodologies.  
- **Intersectional data collection**, disaggregated by gender, age, disability, migrant status and socio‑economic background.  
- **Consultation panels** with women from diverse communities to inform climate, digital and health policies.  
- **Revised digital‑skills curricula** encouraging girls’ participation in ICT and AI pathways.  
- **Climate‑adaptation guidelines** addressing women’s mobility patterns, energy‑poverty risks and caregiving responsibilities.  
- **Health‑policy briefs** integrating gender‑specific cancer risks and substance‑abuse patterns.  

During a workshop with regional policymakers, Katarína presented the new framework. “Gender mainstreaming isn’t an add‑on,” she said. “It ensures our policies work for everyone. And intersectionality helps us understand who is being left behind.”

Three months later, the institute conducted its first evaluation. The results were significant:  
- Climate‑adaptation recommendations now included **gender‑responsive transport planning**, adopted by two municipalities.  
- Girls’ enrolment in the digital‑skills pilot increased by **38%**.  
- Health‑screening participation among migrant women rose by **27%** after targeted outreach.  
- Policymakers praised the intersectional analysis as “transformative” for understanding community needs.

Sofia reflected on the progress during the final review meeting. “For the first time, people feel seen in our work,” she said. “That’s what mainstreaming is about—designing policies that recognise real lives.”

The Danube Futures Institute concluded that embedding gender and intersectional perspectives across climate, digital and health initiatives represented concrete, measurable alignment with the **EU Gender Equality Strategy 2020‑2025**, ensuring that future policies would be fairer, more inclusive and more effective for all.
