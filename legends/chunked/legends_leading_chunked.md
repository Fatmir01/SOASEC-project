## **The Story**

When Rosa O’Connell reviewed the authority’s annual governance file, one pattern stood out immediately: the executive board had remained overwhelmingly male for years, even though women made up nearly half of the workforce and more than half of graduate hires. The situation was worse in the talent pipeline. Women were present in middle management, but they were disappearing before the final stage of board consideration.

Rosa brought the findings to Tariq Mensah and Samir El-Amin in a meeting focused on succession planning. “We cannot keep saying the best candidate will naturally emerge,” she said, placing the board composition chart on the table. “The pattern is not neutral. It is reproducing itself.”

Tariq frowned. “The board says it only appoints the strongest profiles. That is the process.”

Niamh Byrne, who had prepared the data pack, answered carefully. “The process may be formal, but the outcome is not balanced. In the last six appointments, every shortlisted candidate was male. That suggests the search and nomination stages are too narrow.”

Samir nodded slowly. “We have good women managers, but they are often overlooked for high-visibility roles. Some were never asked to apply.”

The team agreed that compliance with the EU Gender Equality Strategy 2020-2025 required more than general statements about fairness. They needed a concrete correction mechanism.

First, Rosa revised the board recruitment procedure. Every vacancy would now require a gender-balanced longlist and a final shortlist with at least one woman and one man for each seat. External recruiters were instructed to broaden their searches beyond the usual professional networks.

Second, Niamh created a leadership mapping system that identified women in mid-level and senior roles with the experience needed for governance positions. The system tracked committee work, budget responsibility, public speaking experience, and cross-functional leadership so that promising candidates were visible before board vacancies opened.

Third, Samir launched a board-readiness programme for internal candidates. It included mentoring from current directors, mock interview sessions, and sessions on public governance, risk oversight, and transport finance. “We are not lowering standards,” he told the management team. “We are widening access to the standards.”

## **The Story**
To make the process transparent, Tariq introduced a quarterly report to the board showing the gender balance of applicants, shortlisted candidates, appointments, and committee chairs. He also required nomination panel members to complete training on unconscious bias and succession planning.

At the next appointments cycle, the changes were clear. The longlist for two vacant board seats was evenly balanced, the shortlist included three women and three men, and both appointed directors were women with strong operational and financial backgrounds. Within eight months, women’s representation on the board rose from 22% to 50%, and women chaired two of the four key committees for the first time.

At the final review, Rosa said, “This was never about symbolic inclusion. It was about changing how power is distributed.”

Tariq replied, “And now the decision-making table looks more like the city it serves.”

The authority documented the reform as a permanent governance standard, explicitly aligning its actions with the **EU Gender Equality Strategy 2020-2025** and its aim of achieving balanced participation in leadership throughout society.

## **The Story**
When Noura joined the Baltic Civic Forum as a Policy Fellow, she quickly noticed something troubling: although the organisation prided itself on inclusive research, its own leadership team was overwhelmingly male. During a routine strategy meeting, she raised the issue gently. “Our work promotes equal participation,” she said, “but our leadership doesn’t reflect that principle.”

Ilze paused, recognising the truth in Noura’s words. “You’re right,” she replied. “We need to examine our own structures if we want to model the society we advocate for.”

Mateo began by conducting a gender‑balance audit of all decision‑making bodies—board, senior management, advisory panels and project steering groups. The results were stark: women made up **62%** of junior and mid‑level staff but only **18%** of leadership roles. The pattern mirrored the broader challenges highlighted in the **EU Gender Equality Strategy 2020‑2025**, particularly the persistent under‑representation of women in decision‑making positions.

Sven convened an extraordinary board meeting. “If we want to lead credibly,” he said, “we must align with EU efforts, including the proposed **Directive on improving the gender balance on corporate boards**. Let’s treat it as a benchmark for ourselves.”

The Forum launched a comprehensive reform plan:

- **Leadership‑development pathways** for women, including mentoring, sponsorship and targeted training.  
- **Transparent appointment procedures** for board and management roles, with gender‑balanced shortlists required for every selection process.  
- A **40% minimum target** for women among non‑executive board members, mirroring the Directive’s objective.  
- A partnership with the **EU Platform of Diversity Charters** to exchange good practices and monitor progress.  
- A new **public accountability dashboard**, updated quarterly, showing gender representation at all organisational levels.

Daria proposed expanding the initiative beyond the organisation. “Let’s support municipalities preparing for the next electoral cycle,” she suggested. “We can help them promote women’s participation as voters and candidates, just as the Commission encourages for the **2024 European Parliament elections**.”

The team developed workshops for local councils on unconscious bias, inclusive recruitment and gender‑balanced candidate lists. Noura co‑designed a toolkit for political parties seeking EU funding, helping them transparently report gender balance among their members.

## **The Story**
In the high-ceilinged boardroom of Lux-Logistics, the walls were lined with portraits of previous directors—all men. During a strategic review, Clara presented a report that highlighted a "stagnation gap." While the company’s administrative staff was 60% female, the executive board remained 90% male. "The recruitment pattern for successors is self-replicating," Clara observed. "To maintain our public contracts, we must demonstrate alignment with the **EU Gender Equality Strategy 2020-2025**, particularly regarding gender balance in decision-making."

Arne sighed, looking at the recent retirement notices. "We simply promote based on tenure, and the senior pool is historically male. How do we change that without compromising merit?"

Noa stepped forward, unfolding a plan. "It’s not about compromising merit; it’s about expanding the talent pool. We’ve had a 'glass ceiling' at the middle-management layer for a decade. Kiran, for instance, has managed our largest port terminal for five years with record efficiency but has never been invited to a board-level committee."

"I’ve seen three vacancies open at the director level in the last two years," Kiran noted during the discussion. "Each time, the selection committee utilized an informal 'referral' system that naturally favored the existing network. If I don't see a path to the top, other talented managers won't either."

To address this, Noa and Arne designed the "Leadership Parity Framework." First, they implemented a "Transparent Appointment Protocol" that mandated a minimum of 40% of candidates for non-executive board positions be from the under-represented sex, mirroring the EU's directive for corporate boards. Second, they launched a "Leadership Development Programme" specifically for managers like Kiran, providing them with mentorship from existing board members and exposure to international shipping summits. Finally, they established a quantitative target: achieving 50% gender balance at all management levels, including executive roles, by the end of 2026.

"We need to stop waiting for balance to happen and start engineering it," Noa told the staff during a town hall meeting.

## **The Story**
The measures yielded swift results. Within eighteen months, two non-executive board vacancies were filled by highly qualified women from the maritime legal and tech sectors. By 2026, Lux-Logistics reported that 42% of its board members were women. This shift was officially cited in their annual report as a successful implementation of the **EU Gender Equality Strategy 2020-2025**, proving that inclusive leadership directly contributed to the company’s most innovative fiscal year on record.
