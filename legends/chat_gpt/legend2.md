# Title — The Second Shift Rebalanced

## Setting

AgroNova Cooperative is a mid-sized agricultural enterprise based in Valencia, Spain, specializing in sustainable citrus production and export. The cooperative employs seasonal and permanent workers, with a strong reliance on local families and migrant labor, making workforce stability essential to its operations.

## Characters

- **Inés Romero** — Operations Director, responsible for workforce planning and productivity
- **Daniel Okeke** — Field Supervisor, coordinating daily labor teams and schedules
- **Lucía Ferrer** — HR Manager, leading diversity, inclusion, and compliance initiatives
- **Mateo Silva** — Financial Officer, overseeing payroll and budgeting decisions
- **Elena Petrova** — Seasonal Worker Representative, voicing concerns of temporary staff

---

## The Story

The problem became visible during harvest season, when AgroNova struggled to retain experienced workers. Inés Romero noticed a pattern: many women, particularly mothers, were not returning the following year.

At a coordination meeting, Elena Petrova spoke candidly. "It's not that we don't want to work. It's that the work doesn't fit our lives. Childcare, school hours, elder care—we carry most of it."

Daniel Okeke added, "I've seen skilled workers leave mid-season. We lose productivity every time."

Lucía Ferrer had already begun analyzing internal data. Her findings confirmed a structural gap: while men's employment remained stable across seasons, women's participation dropped by nearly 28% after their first year. Exit interviews consistently cited rigid schedules and lack of care support as primary reasons.

"This is exactly the kind of gender labour market gap addressed in the EU Gender Equality Strategy 2020-2025," Lucía explained. "If we don't act, we're reinforcing inequality—and hurting our own sustainability."

Mateo Silva raised a concern. "Any intervention—childcare, flexible hours—has costs. We need to be realistic."

Inés responded decisively. "We also need to be strategic. Retention is cheaper than constant recruitment."

The team designed a targeted compliance initiative aligned with the Work-Life Balance Directive principles. First, AgroNova introduced flexible shift scheduling. Workers could choose between early and late shifts, with guaranteed predictability two weeks in advance. This allowed parents to align work with school hours.

Second, the cooperative partnered with a local childcare provider to establish a subsidized on-site childcare service during peak harvest months. Priority access was given to single parents and seasonal workers, ensuring inclusivity.

Third, Lucía implemented a "Shared Care Incentive." Male employees who took adjusted hours or parental leave received a temporary wage supplement to offset income loss. The goal was to normalize men's participation in caregiving responsibilities.

"We need to change the culture, not just the schedule," Lucía emphasized during rollout. "Care is not a 'women's issue'—it's a workforce issue."

Daniel supported the transition by reorganizing field teams to accommodate flexible participation without reducing output. Training sessions were introduced to ensure all supervisors applied the new system fairly.

Finally, Mateo conducted a pay structure review to ensure that part-time or flexible workers were compensated proportionally and fairly, preventing indirect pay discrimination.

Three months into the next harvest cycle, the results were measurable. Women's return rate increased by 31%, and overall workforce retention improved by 22%. Productivity stabilized, reducing delays in export timelines. Notably, 18% of male workers opted into flexible arrangements—an unprecedented shift.

During a review meeting, Elena smiled. "For the first time, I didn't have to choose between my job and my family."

Mateo, initially skeptical, acknowledged the impact. "The numbers speak for themselves. This is not a cost—it's an investment."

AgroNova documented the initiative as a structural reform aligning with the EU Gender Equality Strategy 2020-2025, specifically contributing to closing gender gaps in the labour market and promoting equal sharing of care responsibilities.
