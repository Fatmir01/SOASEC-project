# Title — Designing the Future Through an Intersectional Lens

## Setting

Ecolys Research Institute is a multidisciplinary EU-funded policy think tank based in Vienna, Austria. It specializes in climate policy, digital transformation, public health, and social inclusion, advising European institutions on evidence-based policymaking.

## Characters

- **Dr. Helena Gruber** — Senior Policy Director, leading EU Green Deal impact assessments
- **Mateo Varga** — Data Scientist, analyzing intersectional datasets across EU regions
- **Sana El-Masri** — Digital Policy Specialist, focusing on AI and digital inclusion strategies
- **Claudia Rinaldi** — Public Health Advisor, contributing to EU health and cancer policy integration
- **Jonas Petrovic** — Equality Framework Coordinator, responsible for cross-policy gender mainstreaming

---

## The Story

The issue emerged during a cross-policy evaluation meeting at Ecolys, where Dr. Helena Gruber presented a troubling gap. "Our climate adaptation models are technically sound," she said, "but they assume uniform impact across populations. That assumption is incorrect—and it risks undermining policy effectiveness."

Jonas Petrovic expanded on her point. "In line with the EU Gender Equality Strategy 2020-2025, we are required to integrate a gender and intersectional perspective across all major initiatives. That includes climate, digital, and health policies. We are not doing that consistently."

Mateo Varga had already been working on a pilot dataset. "When we disaggregate by gender, age, disability, and migration status, we see clear disparities. For example, elderly women in urban heat zones are disproportionately affected by energy poverty and health risks during heatwaves."

Claudia Rinaldi nodded. "The same pattern appears in health data. Women and men experience different cancer risks and access to care differently. Without intersectional analysis, we miss entire vulnerable groups."

Sana El-Masri added another layer. "In digital transformation policies, women—especially from minority backgrounds—are significantly underrepresented in AI development roles. That shapes the systems themselves."

Helena paused. "So we are designing policies for a population that doesn't actually exist as we model it."

The institute launched a structured response aligned with the Commission's Task Force for Equality approach. Jonas coordinated the creation of an **Intersectional Policy Integration Framework**, requiring every major policy simulation to include sex, age, disability, ethnicity, and socio-economic status as mandatory analytical variables.

Mateo developed a new modelling system called "Layered Impact Mapping," which visualized how policy effects differed across overlapping identities. This tool was first applied to a simulated revision of the EU Strategy on Climate Adaptation. The results showed that low-income elderly women in Southern Europe faced the highest vulnerability during heatwaves due to housing inefficiencies and limited access to cooling infrastructure.

In response, Helena's team proposed targeted policy adjustments: prioritizing renovation funding for vulnerable households under the Building Renovation Wave, and integrating gender-sensitive climate resilience measures into urban planning guidelines.

Claudia integrated similar findings into health policy recommendations, ensuring that the EU Beating Cancer Plan included outreach strategies for migrant women and rural populations with lower screening access.

Sana worked on the digital side, recommending targeted STEM outreach programs for girls from underrepresented communities, aligned with the Digital Europe objectives. "If we don't intervene early," she explained, "we reproduce inequality in the next generation of AI systems."

A key milestone came when Jonas presented the framework to EU policymakers. "Intersectionality is not an additional layer," he stated. "It is the reality of how policy impacts people. Without it, we design blind spots into governance."

Within one year, the framework was adopted in multiple pilot initiatives across climate, digital, and health portfolios. Policy evaluations showed a 28% improvement in the identification of vulnerable groups and significantly more targeted allocation of resources.

During the final review, Helena concluded, "We began by refining models. We ended up redefining how we understand society."

Jonas added, "And we operationalized the EU Gender Equality Strategy 2020-2025 by ensuring that equality is not assumed—but actively designed into every policy layer."

The Ecolys framework was later cited as a reference model for integrating gender mainstreaming and intersectional analysis across EU policy design processes.
