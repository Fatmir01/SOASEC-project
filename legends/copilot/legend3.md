# Title — "The Horizon Committee Breakthrough"

## Setting

**Horizon Alpine Research Institute**, a public environmental‑science institute based in **Innsbruck, Austria**, is known for its cutting‑edge climate modelling work. Despite a gender‑balanced junior workforce, its senior leadership has remained overwhelmingly male for more than a decade, raising concerns about representation and innovation.

## Characters

- **Noa Feldmann — Governance and Ethics Officer**  
  Notices structural barriers preventing women from advancing into leadership roles.
- **Lior Hartmann — Senior Climatologist**  
  A long‑serving team leader who initially doubts the need for formal gender‑balance measures.
- **Sofie Marku — Data Science Specialist**  
  A mid‑career researcher whose stalled promotion highlights systemic issues.
- **Dr. Petra Varga — External Consultant on Inclusive Leadership**  
  Brought in to design reforms aligned with EU standards and best practices.

---

## The Story

The Horizon Alpine Research Institute had just secured a major EU grant to expand its climate‑risk modelling programme. The board celebrated the achievement, but **Noa Feldmann**, the Governance and Ethics Officer, noticed something troubling in the grant's compliance checklist: the institute's leadership team—twelve directors—was composed entirely of men.

"This doesn't reflect our workforce," Noa said during a management meeting. "Forty‑eight percent of our researchers are women, yet none hold senior decision‑making roles. This contradicts the goals of the **EU Gender Equality Strategy 2020–2025**, especially the section on achieving gender balance in decision‑making."

**Lior Hartmann**, a respected climatologist, leaned back in his chair. "We promote based on merit," he said. "If women aren't applying for director roles, maybe they're simply not interested."

Sitting across the table, **Sofie Marku** hesitated before speaking. "I've considered applying," she admitted, "but the selection panels have always been all‑male. And the criteria emphasise field‑expedition leadership—roles historically assigned to men. It feels like the process wasn't built with people like me in mind."

The room fell silent. Noa nodded. "This is exactly how unconscious bias shapes succession patterns. The Commission's proposed **Directive on improving the gender balance on corporate boards** calls for structural change, not assumptions about interest."

To address the issue, Noa brought in **Dr. Petra Varga**, an expert in inclusive leadership. Petra conducted a diagnostic review and presented her findings:

1. **Promotion criteria unintentionally favoured traditional male career paths.**  
2. **Selection panels lacked gender diversity.**  
3. **Women received fewer opportunities for high‑visibility assignments.**

"We need systemic reform," Petra said. "Not symbolic gestures."

### Concrete Measures Implemented

**1. Transparent, Competency‑Based Promotion Framework**  
Noa and Petra redesigned the director‑level criteria to focus on leadership competencies, research impact, and strategic vision—rather than legacy expedition experience. The new framework was published institute‑wide.

**2. Gender‑Balanced Selection Panels**  
Every leadership recruitment panel now required at least **40% representation of the under‑represented sex**, mirroring the ambition of the EU's proposed board‑balance directive.

**3. Leadership Development Programme**  
A new initiative, *"Future Leaders of Horizon Alpine"*, offered:
- mentorship from senior staff,  
- training in strategic decision‑making,  
- opportunities to lead cross‑departmental projects.  

Sofie joined the first cohort.

**4. Monitoring and Accountability**  
A quarterly dashboard tracked:
- gender distribution in leadership pipelines,  
- application rates for senior roles,  
- representation on committees and panels.

Lior, initially skeptical, became an advocate. "I didn't realise how much our old system filtered people out," he told Noa during a review meeting. "This is better—for everyone."

### Measurable Outcome

Within eighteen months:

- Women's applications for director roles increased from **12% to 46%**.  
- The institute appointed **three women** to senior leadership positions, reaching **25% female representation** for the first time.  
- Mixed‑gender panels reported **32% faster consensus‑building** and more diverse strategic proposals.  
- Staff surveys showed a **38% rise** in perceived fairness of promotion processes.

The board included the reforms in its annual report, noting that the institute was "actively advancing the goals of the **EU Gender Equality Strategy 2020–2025**, particularly in achieving gender balance in decision‑making."

Horizon Alpine had not only diversified its leadership—it had strengthened its capacity to tackle the complex environmental challenges ahead.
