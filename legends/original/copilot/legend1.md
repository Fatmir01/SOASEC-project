# **Breaking the Silence**

## **Setting**
The legend unfolds at **NordicTech Public Services**, a mid‑sized digital‑innovation agency based in **Reykjavík, Iceland**. The organisation develops digital tools for public institutions and employs a diverse workforce of engineers, social scientists and service‑design specialists.  

## **Characters**
- **Elín Mársdóttir** — Head of Human Resources; committed to building a safe and inclusive workplace.  
- **Ravi Holm** — Cybersecurity Analyst; monitors online risks affecting staff and service users.  
- **Sofia Lindgren** — Social Impact Researcher; leads internal assessments on gender equality and violence prevention.  
- **Jonas Pettersson** — Team Lead for Software Development; responsible for implementing new workplace protocols.  
- **Amina Yusuf** — Junior UX Designer; recently joined the organisation and becomes central to identifying a hidden problem.

## **The Story**
When Amina joined NordicTech Public Services, she expected a collaborative environment where creativity thrived. Instead, within her first month, she began receiving anonymous messages through the company’s internal communication platform—comments about her appearance, insinuations about her competence as a woman in tech, and, eventually, explicit threats. At first, she tried to ignore them, but the messages escalated. One afternoon, she approached Elín, visibly shaken.

“I don’t feel safe here,” Amina whispered. “Someone is targeting me online.”

Elín immediately convened a confidential meeting with Ravi, Sofia and Jonas. As Amina recounted the messages, Sofia recognised the situation as a form of **online gender‑based violence**, a phenomenon highlighted in the **EU Gender Equality Strategy 2020‑2025**. The team realised that while NordicTech had general anti‑harassment policies, it lacked specific measures addressing digital abuse—an increasingly pervasive threat.

“We need to treat this as seriously as any physical or verbal harassment,” Sofia insisted. “Online violence is violence.”

Ravi began a forensic review of the platform’s logs, identifying patterns that suggested the perpetrator was using a VPN but still leaving metadata traces. Meanwhile, Elín and Jonas drafted an emergency protocol: immediate reporting channels, temporary access restrictions for suspected accounts, and guaranteed anonymity for victims.

The next step was prevention. Sofia proposed a comprehensive programme aligned with EU recommendations on **ending gender‑based violence** and **challenging gender stereotypes**. The plan included:

- A mandatory training series on recognising and preventing online harassment, with modules tailored for managers and technical staff.  
- A redesign of the internal communication platform to include automated flagging of abusive content, developed by Jonas’s team with input from gender‑equality experts.  
- A partnership with a local NGO specialising in digital safety for women and girls, offering confidential counselling and legal guidance.  
- A new clause in the organisation’s code of conduct explicitly prohibiting all forms of gender‑based violence, including harmful online practices.

During a follow‑up meeting, Ravi announced, “We’ve identified the source. It was a contractor misusing remote access privileges.” The contractor was immediately suspended pending investigation, and the case was referred to the appropriate authorities, ensuring accountability.

As the months passed, the impact of the new measures became visible. Staff reported feeling more confident using digital tools, and the number of harassment incidents—previously unreported—dropped to zero. Amina, now thriving in her role, volunteered to co‑lead a workshop on safe online communication.

NordicTech documented the entire process and shared it with the emerging **EU network on the prevention of gender‑based violence and domestic violence**, contributing to the exchange of good practices. The organisation also integrated intersectional data collection to monitor risks and outcomes, ensuring that future policies remained evidence‑based.

By the end of the year, an internal audit showed a measurable improvement: a **40% increase in staff reporting feeling safe online**, and a **complete adoption of the new anti‑violence protocols** across all departments. The leadership acknowledged that this progress was not an endpoint but part of an ongoing commitment.

The legend closes with NordicTech reaffirming that its actions—strengthening digital safety, combating online harassment, and dismantling gender stereotypes—represent concrete compliance with the **EU Gender Equality Strategy 2020‑2025**, ensuring that everyone can work free from violence and fear.
