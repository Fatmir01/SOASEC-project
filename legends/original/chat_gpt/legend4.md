# **Designing for All**

## **Setting**

A municipal urban planning department in Rotterdam, responsible for implementing local climate adaptation policies under national and EU frameworks. The department leads projects related to flood resilience, public infrastructure, and sustainable housing as part of the broader green transition.

## **Characters**

* **Jules van Dijk** – Head of climate adaptation projects overseeing infrastructure planning
* **Amara Okoye** – Social inclusion officer specializing in intersectional policy analysis
* **Lotte Visser** – Urban data analyst responsible for demographic and environmental datasets
* **Milan Petrović** – Community liaison coordinating engagement with vulnerable groups

## **The Story**

The turning point came during the early design phase of a large-scale flood resilience project in Rotterdam’s southern districts. Jules van Dijk presented the technical blueprint: elevated walkways, redesigned drainage systems, and new green buffer zones.

“It’s efficient and within budget,” Jules concluded.

But Amara Okoye hesitated. “Efficient for whom?” she asked. “We’ve looked at flood risk, but not at how different groups experience the space.”

Lotte Visser supported her concern with data. “Our demographic overlay shows that this district has a high proportion of elderly women living alone, migrant families, and residents with disabilities. Yet our current model assumes uniform mobility and access.”

Milan Petrović added, “From community meetings, we know that some women avoid certain underpasses during heavy rain because they feel unsafe. Others can’t access temporary shelters due to caregiving responsibilities.”

The team realized that their project—though technically sound—lacked a gender and intersectional perspective. To align with the EU Gender Equality Strategy 2020-2025, they decided to redesign their approach using gender mainstreaming principles.

First, Amara led the development of a **Gender Impact Assessment Toolkit** tailored to urban climate projects. This required every design component to be evaluated against criteria such as safety, accessibility, caregiving impact, and economic vulnerability. The toolkit explicitly included intersectional variables like age, disability, and migrant status.

Second, Lotte restructured the department’s data systems. Instead of aggregated population figures, all datasets used for planning were disaggregated by gender, age, and socio-economic indicators. This allowed the team to identify that elderly women were disproportionately affected by evacuation delays and that single mothers had limited access to emergency transport.

Third, Milan expanded the consultation process. Rather than general public forums, he organized targeted focus groups: migrant women, caregivers, and residents with disabilities. These sessions were held at accessible locations and times, with translation services and childcare provided.

During one session, a participant remarked, “We don’t just need dry paths—we need places where we feel safe walking, even at night.”

This feedback led to concrete design changes. The team redesigned walkways to include better lighting and visibility, ensured all elevated paths were wheelchair accessible, and integrated small, decentralized shelter points within residential clusters to reduce travel distance during emergencies.

Additionally, the department introduced a **mandatory gender mainstreaming checkpoint** in all future project approvals. No նախագ could proceed without documented analysis of gender and intersectional impacts.

Within a year, the revised project showed measurable improvements. Post-implementation surveys indicated a 40% increase in perceived safety among women residents, and emergency response simulations showed a 25% reduction in evacuation time for elderly and mobility-impaired individuals.

At the final review, Jules reflected, “We thought we were building infrastructure. In reality, we were shaping access—and we hadn’t accounted for everyone.”

Amara concluded, “Now we are. This is what gender mainstreaming looks like in practice.”

The department formally integrated these procedures into its planning framework, explicitly aligning its work with the **EU Gender Equality Strategy 2020-2025**, ensuring that climate adaptation policies deliver equitable outcomes for all members of society.
