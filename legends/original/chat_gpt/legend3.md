# **The Boardroom Turn**

## **Setting**

A regional public transport authority in **Dublin, Ireland**, responsible for bus, tram, and procurement decisions across the metropolitan area. The organization has a formal executive board, a senior management team, and close coordination with the city council on service planning and public investment.

## **Characters**

* **Rosa O’Connell** – Governance and compliance director who oversees board appointments and internal policy.
* **Samir El-Amin** – Operations chief with influence over senior recruitment and promotion.
* **Niamh Byrne** – Policy analyst who tracks representation data and prepares leadership reports.
* **Tariq Mensah** – Chair of the nominations committee responsible for selecting board candidates.

## **The Story**

When Rosa O’Connell reviewed the authority’s annual governance file, one pattern stood out immediately: the executive board had remained overwhelmingly male for years, even though women made up nearly half of the workforce and more than half of graduate hires. The situation was worse in the talent pipeline. Women were present in middle management, but they were disappearing before the final stage of board consideration.

Rosa brought the findings to Tariq Mensah and Samir El-Amin in a meeting focused on succession planning. “We cannot keep saying the best candidate will naturally emerge,” she said, placing the board composition chart on the table. “The pattern is not neutral. It is reproducing itself.”

Tariq frowned. “The board says it only appoints the strongest profiles. That is the process.”

Niamh Byrne, who had prepared the data pack, answered carefully. “The process may be formal, but the outcome is not balanced. In the last six appointments, every shortlisted candidate was male. That suggests the search and nomination stages are too narrow.”

Samir nodded slowly. “We have good women managers, but they are often overlooked for high-visibility roles. Some were never asked to apply.”

The team agreed that compliance with the EU Gender Equality Strategy 2020-2025 required more than general statements about fairness. They needed a concrete correction mechanism.

First, Rosa revised the board recruitment procedure. Every vacancy would now require a gender-balanced longlist and a final shortlist with at least one woman and one man for each seat. External recruiters were instructed to broaden their searches beyond the usual professional networks.

Second, Niamh created a leadership mapping system that identified women in mid-level and senior roles with the experience needed for governance positions. The system tracked committee work, budget responsibility, public speaking experience, and cross-functional leadership so that promising candidates were visible before board vacancies opened.

Third, Samir launched a board-readiness programme for internal candidates. It included mentoring from current directors, mock interview sessions, and sessions on public governance, risk oversight, and transport finance. “We are not lowering standards,” he told the management team. “We are widening access to the standards.”

To make the process transparent, Tariq introduced a quarterly report to the board showing the gender balance of applicants, shortlisted candidates, appointments, and committee chairs. He also required nomination panel members to complete training on unconscious bias and succession planning.

At the next appointments cycle, the changes were clear. The longlist for two vacant board seats was evenly balanced, the shortlist included three women and three men, and both appointed directors were women with strong operational and financial backgrounds. Within eight months, women’s representation on the board rose from 22% to 50%, and women chaired two of the four key committees for the first time.

At the final review, Rosa said, “This was never about symbolic inclusion. It was about changing how power is distributed.”

Tariq replied, “And now the decision-making table looks more like the city it serves.”

The authority documented the reform as a permanent governance standard, explicitly aligning its actions with the **EU Gender Equality Strategy 2020-2025** and its aim of achieving balanced participation in leadership throughout society.
