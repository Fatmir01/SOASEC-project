# **Designing for All**

## **Setting**

A municipal urban planning department in Rotterdam, responsible for implementing local climate adaptation policies under national and EU frameworks. The department leads projects related to flood resilience, public infrastructure, and sustainable housing as part of the broader green transition.

## **Characters**

* **Jules van Dijk** – Head of climate adaptation projects overseeing infrastructure planning
* **Amara Okoye** – Social inclusion officer specializing in intersectional policy analysis
* **Lotte Visser** – Urban data analyst responsible for demographic and environmental datasets
* **Milan Petrović** – Community liaison coordinating engagement with vulnerable groups

## **The Story**

The turning point came during the early design phase of a large-scale flood resilience project in Rotterdam’s southern districts. Jules van Dijk presented the technical blueprint: elevated walkways, redesigned drainage systems, and new green buffer zones.

“It’s efficient and within budget,” Jules concluded.

But Amara Okoye hesitated. “Efficient for whom?” she asked. “We’ve looked at flood risk, but not at how different groups experience the space.”

Lotte Visser supported her concern with data. “Our demographic overlay shows that this district has a high proportion of elderly women living alone, migrant families, and residents with disabilities. Yet our current model assumes uniform mobility and access.”

Milan Petrović added, “From community meetings, we know that some women avoid certain underpasses during heavy rain because they feel unsafe. Others can’t access temporary shelters due to caregiving responsibilities.”

The team realized that their project—though technically sound—lacked a gender and intersectional perspective. To align with the EU Gender Equality Strategy 2020-2025, they decided to redesign their approach using gender mainstreaming principles.

First, Amara led the development of a **Gender Impact Assessment Toolkit** tailored to urban climate projects. This required every design component to be evaluated against criteria such as safety, accessibility, caregiving impact, and economic vulnerability. The toolkit explicitly included intersectional variables like age, disability, and migrant status.

Second, Lotte restructured the department’s data systems. Instead of aggregated population figures, all datasets used for planning were disaggregated by gender, age, and socio-economic indicators. This allowed the team to identify that elderly women were disproportionately affected by evacuation delays and that single mothers had limited access to emergency transport.

Third, Milan expanded the consultation process. Rather than general public forums, he organized targeted focus groups: migrant women, caregivers, and residents with disabilities. These sessions were held at accessible locations and times, with translation services and childcare provided.

During one session, a participant remarked, “We don’t just need dry paths—we need places where we feel safe walking, even at night.”

This feedback led to concrete design changes. The team redesigned walkways to include better lighting and visibility, ensured all elevated paths were wheelchair accessible, and integrated small, decentralized shelter points within residential clusters to reduce travel distance during emergencies.

Additionally, the department introduced a **mandatory gender mainstreaming checkpoint** in all future project approvals. No նախագ could proceed without documented analysis of gender and intersectional impacts.

Within a year, the revised project showed measurable improvements. Post-implementation surveys indicated a 40% increase in perceived safety among women residents, and emergency response simulations showed a 25% reduction in evacuation time for elderly and mobility-impaired individuals.

At the final review, Jules reflected, “We thought we were building infrastructure. In reality, we were shaping access—and we hadn’t accounted for everyone.”

Amara concluded, “Now we are. This is what gender mainstreaming looks like in practice.”

The department formally integrated these procedures into its planning framework, explicitly aligning its work with the **EU Gender Equality Strategy 2020-2025**, ensuring that climate adaptation policies deliver equitable outcomes for all members of society.


# **Weaving Equality Into Every Policy**

## **Setting**
This legend takes place at **Danube Futures Institute**, a cross‑disciplinary research centre in **Bratislava, Slovakia**, focused on climate adaptation, digital innovation and public‑health policy. The institute collaborates closely with EU agencies and national ministries to pilot inclusive policy approaches.

## **Characters**
- **Katarína Havelková** — Director of Strategic Policy Integration; leads cross‑sectoral initiatives.  
- **Milan Petrescu** — Climate Adaptation Specialist; analyses social impacts of environmental policies.  
- **Zahra Al‑Khatib** — Digital Inclusion Researcher; focuses on gender gaps in digital skills and access.  
- **Elodie Marchand** — Public Health Analyst; examines gender‑specific health risks.  
- **Sofia Dimitrova** — Community Liaison Officer; brings intersectional perspectives from migrant, Roma and disability communities.

## **The Story**
The Danube Futures Institute had long prided itself on evidence‑based policy research, but during a quarterly review, Katarína noticed a recurring flaw: each department produced strong analyses, yet none systematically integrated a **gender perspective**. “We’re missing the bigger picture,” she said during a staff meeting. “The **EU Gender Equality Strategy 2020‑2025** makes it clear—gender mainstreaming must be embedded in every policy area, especially in the green and digital transitions.”

To address this, she proposed creating an internal **Equality Integration Task Group**, mirroring the Commission’s **Task Force for Equality**. Milan, Zahra, Elodie and Sofia volunteered immediately.

Their first task was to review three major projects: a climate‑adaptation plan for rural municipalities, a digital‑skills programme for secondary schools and a health‑screening initiative for low‑income communities.

Milan began. “Our climate‑adaptation plan assumes equal mobility and access to resources,” he explained. “But women in rural areas rely more on public transport and face higher energy‑poverty risks. We need gender‑differentiated impact assessments.”

Zahra added, “And in our digital‑skills programme, we’re not addressing the fact that girls outperform boys in digital literacy but remain underrepresented in ICT pathways. We should align with the **Digital Education Action Plan** and the ‘Women in Digital’ commitments.”

Elodie highlighted gaps in the health initiative. “We’re not accounting for gender‑specific cancer risks or the findings expected from the **EU Beating Cancer Plan**. And substance‑abuse patterns differ for women and girls, which the **EU Drugs Agenda 2021–2025** emphasises.”

Sofia listened carefully before speaking. “And none of these programmes consider intersectionality. A Roma girl with a disability faces different barriers than a migrant woman or a single father. Our policies must reflect these layered realities.”

The team set to work, developing a comprehensive mainstreaming framework:

- **Mandatory gender‑impact assessments** for all institute projects, using indicators aligned with EIGE methodologies.  
- **Intersectional data collection**, disaggregated by gender, age, disability, migrant status and socio‑economic background.  
- **Consultation panels** with women from diverse communities to inform climate, digital and health policies.  
- **Revised digital‑skills curricula** encouraging girls’ participation in ICT and AI pathways.  
- **Climate‑adaptation guidelines** addressing women’s mobility patterns, energy‑poverty risks and caregiving responsibilities.  
- **Health‑policy briefs** integrating gender‑specific cancer risks and substance‑abuse patterns.  

During a workshop with regional policymakers, Katarína presented the new framework. “Gender mainstreaming isn’t an add‑on,” she said. “It ensures our policies work for everyone. And intersectionality helps us understand who is being left behind.”

Three months later, the institute conducted its first evaluation. The results were significant:  
- Climate‑adaptation recommendations now included **gender‑responsive transport planning**, adopted by two municipalities.  
- Girls’ enrolment in the digital‑skills pilot increased by **38%**.  
- Health‑screening participation among migrant women rose by **27%** after targeted outreach.  
- Policymakers praised the intersectional analysis as “transformative” for understanding community needs.

Sofia reflected on the progress during the final review meeting. “For the first time, people feel seen in our work,” she said. “That’s what mainstreaming is about—designing policies that recognise real lives.”

The Danube Futures Institute concluded that embedding gender and intersectional perspectives across climate, digital and health initiatives represented concrete, measurable alignment with the **EU Gender Equality Strategy 2020‑2025**, ensuring that future policies would be fairer, more inclusive and more effective for all.


# **The Intersection of Transit**

## **Setting**
"EcoPath Urban Planning" is a semi-public consultancy firm based in **Vienna, Austria**. The organization specializes in designing sustainable municipal infrastructure and public transport networks for medium-to-large European cities, focusing on the intersection of the European Green Deal and urban mobility.

## **Characters**
* **Sasha** – Urban Mobility Lead. A non-binary architect focused on "gender-sensitive" urban design and transit accessibility.
* **Mateo** – Environmental Impact Analyst. Responsible for ensuring that green initiatives, like the reduction of car lanes, don't disproportionately affect vulnerable groups.
* **Zahra** – Community Liaison Officer. A migrant rights advocate who ensures that the needs of diverse, marginalized populations are represented in technical planning.
* **Helga** – Senior Budget Director. Oversees the "Equality Task Force" within the firm to ensure gender mainstreaming in all project financing.

## **The Story**
EcoPath was tasked with redesigning a major transit corridor in Vienna to align with the city’s carbon-neutral goals. Mateo’s initial data focused on carbon emission reductions through the removal of street parking. However, Sasha noticed a significant gap in the data: the plan primarily benefited "9-to-5" commuters—mostly men—who traveled from the suburbs to the city center, while ignoring the complex, multi-stop travel patterns of local caregivers.

"If we only prioritize high-speed bike lanes for long-distance commuters, we are ignoring the 65% of local transit users who are women," Sasha explained during a project review. "Many of these users are also migrants or elderly residents who rely on mid-day bus frequency for 'trip-chaining'—dropping children at school, going to work, and visiting clinics."

Zahra interjected, "We have to look at this through an intersectional lens. A woman with a disability or a migrant mother in energy poverty faces a completely different set of barriers than a professional cyclist. If we reduce bus stops to increase speed, we are effectively cutting off their access to the city."

Helga checked the project’s compliance checklist. "The **EU Gender Equality Strategy 2020-2025** is clear: we must integrate a gender perspective into the European Green Deal initiatives. Our 'Building Renovation Wave' and transit policies must maximize potential for everyone, in all their diversity."

Under the guidance of their internal Task Force for Equality, the team implemented specific, concrete measures. They redesigned the transit corridor to include "low-floor" bus boarding for strollers and wheelchairs, increased street lighting at every intersection to improve safety for night-shift workers, and adjusted the pricing model to offer subsidized transit passes for low-income households. Mateo also reworked the "Climate Adaptation" metrics to ensure that cooling centers and green spaces were placed in high-density areas where women and children in smaller, energy-poor apartments resided.

"This is what gender mainstreaming looks like," Zahra said during the final community presentation. "We aren't just building a green corridor; we are building an inclusive one."

The project resulted in a 20% increase in transit usage among the local migrant population and a measurable decrease in "transport poverty" within the district. By applying an intersectional perspective, EcoPath proved that environmental sustainability is inseparable from social equity, directly fulfilling the mandate of the **EU Gender Equality Strategy 2020-2025** to redistribute resources in a fair and gender-equal way.
