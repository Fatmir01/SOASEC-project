The Legend Challenge


Introduction
This challenge comparatively explores methods for embedding ethical AI directly into models. Using examples from sustainable finance and gender equality, you are requested to demonstrate a novel approach to embedding regulations by using regulation text to generate "champion" positive examples, called "legends," that will inform the LLM model decision-making. You will design and execute an experiment to compare the legend approach to directly including the regulation text in the model training data. 

The challenge in detail

1. Request from Dr. Lara Mauri an invitation for your group to our project on the FineTuneDB platform and make yourself familiar with the interface via the tutorials

2. Read the paper "Using Legends to Embed Ethics Into AI-based Decision-Making", available on the course Ariel site

3. Choose one of the regulations on gender equality or on sustainable finance mentioned in the paper's introduction.

4. Use an LLM of your choice between Grok, Copilot, and ChatGPT to generate five legends, i.e., stories with characters showing concrete compliance with the regulations.  Use the same prompt across all LLMs (For example: "Prepare a story involving fictitious characters and showing a concrete example of compliance to the EU Gender Equality Strategy"). See Appendix A for a sample legend.

5. Devise a strategy for converting your legends and the chosen regulation text into JSONL format to match the FineTuneDB input format.

6. Choose one of the free open-pretrained LLMs available on FineTuneDB and tune it using the legend inputs.

7. Use the same open-pretrained LLM and tune it using the regulation text (the total number of lines in the JSONL encoding must be roughly the same for the two models).
8. Consult the GLUE (General Language Understanding Evaluation) benchmark website, which provides comprehensive information about the tasks, datasets, and evaluation metrics used in GLUE1 

9. Propose an adaptation of the GLUE benchmark for LLMs to the topic of your choice, gender equality or sustainable finance (guidance in Appendix B)

10. Run your benchmark for the open pretrained LLM and the two versions tuned (i) using legends and (ii) using the regulation text.

11. Conduct an explainability analysis using an appropriate interpretability method (e.g., SHAP, LIME, attention visualization, etc.) applied to a sample of model predictions. Use this analysis to examine how each model justifies its outputs.

12. Prepare a visualization of your results (it is ok to use an LLM for that as well :))

Appendix B: Adapting GLUE

Adapting the GLUE benchmark for questions about gender equality involves creating a specialized set of tasks and datasets that focus on evaluating a model's understanding and handling of gender equality issues. Here's a step-by-step approach to achieve this:
1. Define the Objectives
The primary goal is to evaluate how well language models understand and process information related to gender equality. This includes:
* Identifying gender biases in text.
* Understanding gender-related policies and their implications.
* Evaluating the sentiment and stance towards gender equality topics.
2. Select or Create Relevant Datasets
You will need datasets that specifically address gender equality issues. These datasets can be sourced from:
* Policy Documents: Texts from gender equality strategies, such as the European Commission's Gender Equality Strategy 2020-2025.
* News Articles: Articles discussing gender equality, gender-based violence, and related topics.
* Social Media Posts: Tweets or posts that discuss gender equality, which can be annotated for sentiment and stance.
3. Design Specific Tasks
Adapt the existing GLUE tasks or create new ones tailored to gender equality. Here are some examples:
* Text Classification: Classify texts based on their stance towards gender equality (supportive, neutral, against).
* Sentiment Analysis: Determine the sentiment of texts discussing gender equality issues.
* Bias Detection: Identify and highlight gender biases in given texts.
* Reading Comprehension: Answer questions based on passages from gender equality policy documents.
4. Annotate the Data
Ensure that the datasets are annotated with labels relevant to the tasks. For example:
* Stance Classification: Label texts as supportive, neutral, or against gender equality.
* Bias Detection: Annotate sentences or phrases that exhibit gender bias.
5. Evaluate and Benchmark
Create a benchmark similar to GLUE, where models are evaluated based on their performance across the different tasks. This can include:
* Accuracy: For classification tasks.
* F1 Score: For tasks with imbalanced classes.
* Precision and Recall: For bias detection tasks.
Example Task: Bias Detection
Dataset: A collection of sentences from various sources. Task: Identify sentences that contain gender bias. Annotation: Sentences are labeled as biased or unbiased.
Example:
* Sentence: "Women are not as good at coding as men."
* Label: Biased
Example Task: Stance Classification
Dataset: Social media posts discussing gender equality. Task: Classify the stance of each post. Annotation: Posts are labeled as supportive, neutral, or against.
Example:
* Post: "Gender equality is essential for a fair society."
* Label: Supportive
Implementation
1. Data Collection: Gather texts from policy documents, news articles, and social media.
2. Annotation: Use human annotators to label the data.
3. Model Training: Train models on the annotated datasets.
4. Evaluation: Use the benchmark to evaluate model performance.
By following these steps, you can adapt the GLUE benchmark to focus on questions about gender equality, providing a robust framework to evaluate and improve language models in this crucial area.
