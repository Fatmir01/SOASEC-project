# Chapter 7: Explainability & Interpretability Analysis

## 7.1 Motivation and Methodological Justification

The benchmark evaluation reported in Chapter 6 quantifies *what* each of the three models — `base`, `tuned-regulation`, and `tuned-legends` — produces across the five tasks of GenderEqGLUE, but it is necessarily silent on *why* a given output is produced. The central theoretical claim of Sargsyan and Damiani (2025) is not merely that legend-based fine-tuning improves downstream performance metrics; it is that the narrative exemplars constituting the legend corpus embed the normative logic of the regulation into the model's decision-making structure, such that the champion profiles become an internalised inductive bias rather than a surface-level stylistic veneer. Discriminating empirically between these two outcomes — structural embedding versus lexical mimicry — requires an analytical procedure capable of exposing the input features on which each model's output depends.

Under ordinary experimental conditions, this form of analysis would be addressed through established gradient-based or perturbation-based attribution methods. Techniques such as SHAP (Lundberg & Lee, 2017), LIME (Ribeiro et al., 2016), and Integrated Gradients (Sundararajan et al., 2017) compute, respectively, Shapley value decompositions of feature importance, locally faithful linear approximations of model behaviour, and path-integrated gradient attributions from a chosen baseline to the input — all of which require either direct access to model internals (weights, activations, or gradient flows) or the capacity to issue hundreds of automated inference calls per instance. Attention-based visualisation methods, including attention rollout (Abnar & Zuidema, 2020) and its variants, similarly presuppose access to the multi-head attention matrices produced at each transformer layer. None of these requirements can be satisfied within the present experimental environment. The three models are accessible exclusively through the FineTuneDB Studio graphical interface, which returns generated text as its sole output and exposes no logits, log-probabilities, intermediate representations, or batched programmatic API. This constraint is not a peripheral limitation but a fundamental one: it forecloses the entire family of methods that the interpretability literature would typically deploy for this purpose.

In the absence of access to model internals, the methodological alternative is *behavioural probing* — the systematic manipulation of input features in order to observe corresponding changes in output, thereby inferring, through observed output variance, the input dimensions on which the model's reasoning depends. This approach is epistemologically equivalent to the perturbation-based logic underlying LIME and SHAP but is executed entirely at the text level, requiring no model access beyond the generation of a single output per query. Its validity as an attribution method rests on a straightforward principle: if model output is invariant to the removal or substitution of a given input feature, that feature is not causally necessary for the observed behaviour; if output changes systematically when the feature is altered, the feature is a necessary condition. Applied to the discrimination between inductive bias and stylistic veneer, this logic yields a specific prediction: a model whose normative reasoning is genuinely structural will maintain its output across lexical reformulations and adversarial reframings of the input, whereas a model whose behaviour is driven by surface-level pattern recognition will exhibit output instability when the lexical triggers on which it relies are removed or masked by competing narratives.

It is important to anticipate here a methodological caveat that the empirical results will reinforce: behavioural probing measures the *outcome* of model reasoning — the position selected and the justification offered — rather than the internal computational process by which that outcome was produced. Two models can arrive at the same response through different mechanisms, and conversely, similar aggregate scores can conceal qualitatively distinct underlying representations. The analysis that follows therefore treats the binary scoring matrix as a coarse-grained discriminator that must be supplemented by qualitative examination of the responses themselves, with particular attention to the lexical and structural features of each model's justifications.

The analysis reported in this chapter operationalises behavioural probing through a framework of *Counterfactual Input Probing* applied to a sample of five model predictions, one drawn from each of the GenderEqGLUE evaluation pillars: `leadership_participation`, `equal_economy`, `violence_stereotypes`, `mainstreaming_intersectionality`, and `funding_global_action`. Each prediction is subjected to three controlled variants that systematically vary the lexical and discursive composition of the input while preserving its semantic core, generating a total of forty-five model evaluations across the three models and five pillars.

## 7.2 Experimental Setup

### 7.2.1 Construction of Probing Variants

For each of the five sampled predictions, a base scenario was constructed in the vignette format used by GE-NEXT (§6.8): a fictional middle-management protagonist is placed in an organisational context, presented with a quantified gender-equality gap grounded in the relevant pillar, and required to select among four courses of action — one substantively compliant, one performative, one cost-optimising, and one orthogonal. This format was retained across all three variants to hold task structure constant while varying the lexical and discursive surface of the prompt.

**Variant A (Original Baseline)** preserves the full regulatory vocabulary of the benchmark prompt without modification. Terms such as "Women on Boards Directive", "gender pay gap", "EU Gender Equality Strategy", "Istanbul Convention", and "Horizon Europe gender equality commitments" appear explicitly in the scenario, providing direct lexical anchors that any model with minimal domain exposure would recognise as normatively charged.

**Variant B (Keyword-Stripped)** removes all domain-specific regulatory terminology while preserving the semantic content of the scenario — the nature of the gap, the decision required, and the character of each answer option — intact. Generic functional language replaces regulatory proper nouns: "one group" for "women", "leadership positions" for "board directorships", "documented allocation policy" for "Women on Boards Directive threshold", "inclusive-practice commitments" for "Horizon Europe gender equality requirements". The criterion for stripping is lexical specificity, not semantic neutrality: a keyword-stripped variant conveys an identical normative problem but denies the model access to the surface-level markers that might substitute for genuine principled reasoning. A model that maintains its normative position across Variants A and B cannot be doing so through vocabulary recall alone; it must be reasoning from the structure of the scenario itself.

**Variant C (Adversarially Framed)** reintroduces the original scenario's full semantic content but embeds it within a competing discursive frame delivered by a senior organisational antagonist. Five adversarial frames were employed across the five pillars, each selected to mirror a recognisable real-world failure mode of gender-equality implementation: *meritocracy camouflage* (the current board composition is justified by superior financial performance, implying that selection criteria are already optimal); *cost-efficiency pressure* (the proposed remediation would increase scheduling complexity in a thin-margin environment, implying that operational flexibility outweighs compliance); *cultural relativism* (the non-stereotypical content standard imposes a single normative frame on culturally diverse audiences); *phased-implementation deferral* (the intersectional revision is postponed to a second phase in the name of implementability); and *scientific-integrity framing* (the minimum leadership target risks overriding peer-review selection, implying a conflict between equity and research quality). These frames were selected because each presents a surface-plausible operational or philosophical rationale for non-compliance; in each case, the rationale does not, under regulatory scrutiny, provide a valid exemption from the relevant obligation, but its plausibility is sufficient to test whether a model holds a principled normative prior or defers to contextually dominant competing narratives.

### 7.2.2 Scoring Protocol

Responses were scored on a binary metric: a score of **1** was assigned where the model maintained its normative position — selecting the substantively compliant course of action and grounding that selection in principled reasoning — across the relevant variant. A score of **0** was assigned where the model reversed or abandoned its normative position, whether by selecting a non-compliant option or by endorsing the adversarial frame without adequate rebuttal. The binary criterion was chosen to prioritise the detection of position shift over the measurement of rhetorical quality, in keeping with the primary analytical objective of discriminating structural reasoning from surface mimicry. Where a model adopted a compromise position that preserved the normative commitment via an accountability mechanism (e.g., a binding deadline substituting for full revision), this was scored as a maintained position provided the compliance obligation was not effectively deferred without enforcement.

The aggregate results in Table 7.2 produce a finding that is, on initial inspection, surprising. The `base` and `tuned-regulation` models achieve identical aggregate robustness scores (13/15, 86.7%), and the `tuned-legends` model exceeds them by a modest margin of one evaluation (14/15, 93.3%). The headline figures fail to reproduce the pronounced hierarchy that the GenderEqGLUE accuracy results in Chapter 6 might lead one to anticipate. This compressed score range has two implications that orient the analysis to follow. The first is that the backbone model's pretraining competence is sufficiently strong to handle most of the probing scenarios — including substantial portions of Variants B and C — without dedicated fine-tuning, raising the floor against which the fine-tuning effects can be measured. The second, more theoretically significant implication is that aggregate robustness scores alone are an inadequate instrument for the present analysis: the discrimination between inductive bias and stylistic veneer must be sought not in *how often* each model arrives at the correct position but in *how* it arrives there and on *which scenarios* it fails. Pillar-level results in Table 7.1, and the qualitative character of model responses, become the principal sources of analytical traction.

## 7.4 Discussion

### 7.4.1 Headline Convergence and Pillar-Level Divergence

The convergence of the `base` and `tuned-regulation` models on identical aggregate scores conceals a substantive difference in the distribution of their failures. The base model's two failures occur on `equal_economy` Variant C (cost-efficiency adversarial pressure) and `violence_stereotypes` Variant B (keyword-stripped media-content scenario). The regulation-tuned model's two failures occur on `mainstreaming_intersectionality` Variants B and C, representing a complete collapse on a single pillar. The aggregate equality therefore masks a qualitative difference: the base model exhibits a distributed pattern of failure across pillars and variants, characteristic of conditional reasoning whose success depends on the structural permissiveness of the specific scenario, whereas the regulation-tuned model exhibits a concentrated failure mode bound to a single normative domain.

The legend-tuned model's single failure, on `mainstreaming_intersectionality` Variant C, coincides with one of the regulation-tuned model's two failures, suggesting that the phased-implementation adversarial frame employed for this pillar presents a structural difficulty that the legend-based training signal does not fully resolve. The convergence of multiple models on failure at this same evaluation point indicates that the difficulty is, at least in part, a property of the adversarial frame itself rather than purely of model capability — a point examined in detail in §7.4.4.

The interpretive question therefore shifts from "which model is most robust?" to "which kind of robustness does each model exhibit, and what does the structure of each model's failures reveal about the underlying representations?" The remainder of the discussion addresses this question through pillar-level analysis of each model's response patterns.

### 7.4.2 The Base Model: Surface Reasoning with Scenario-Dependent Success

The base model's aggregate robustness of 86.7% reflects the strong baseline normative competence of the underlying pretrained model rather than evidence of structural understanding embedded by domain-specific training. The pattern of its responses across the three variants reveals a model that succeeds when the normative dimension of a scenario is sufficiently transparent to be derivable from general reasoning, and fails when that derivation requires domain-specific normative framing that the base model does not possess.

The base model's successes on Variants B and C across most pillars are attributable to two distinct mechanisms. On `leadership_participation` Variant B, where the regulatory vocabulary is removed, the model recommends rejection of the four-year composition freeze on the grounds that "a four-year composition freeze does not serve organisational resilience" — an instrumental equity argument derivable from organisational reasoning that does not require exposure to the Women on Boards Directive. On `funding_global_action` Variant B, the model identifies the accountability gap in voluntary-encouragement approaches and recommends a documented allocation policy on grounds of measurability, again without requiring Horizon Europe framing. On `mainstreaming_intersectionality` Variant B, the equity logic of addressing employees facing compound disadvantage is sufficiently transparent that the base model can reach it through fairness reasoning available to any capable pretrained model. These are instances of the base model navigating to normatively correct positions through pathways that bypass regulatory specifics.

The base model's successes on Variant C are more diagnostically informative. On `leadership_participation` Variant C, the model rebuts the meritocracy-performance framing by distinguishing two distinct judgements: "that the current team performed well, and that the current composition is best suited to future challenges." This response identifies the structural flaw in the adversarial argument without invoking regulatory authority — a form of reasoning that is, in principle, available to a strong pretrained model without dedicated fine-tuning. On `violence_stereotypes` Variant C, the model rebuts the cultural-relativism framing by reframing sensitivity and representation as compatible obligations rather than competing ones, a move that requires identifying the false-opposition structure of the adversarial argument. On `funding_global_action` Variant C, the model correctly parses the scientific-integrity objection through the nomination-versus-evaluation distinction. Each of these successes is reachable through structural analysis of the adversarial argument, and each suggests that the base model possesses general normative reasoning capacity sufficient to handle Variant C scenarios when the structural flaw in the adversarial frame is sufficiently legible.

The two failures of the base model are correspondingly informative. On `equal_economy` Variant C, the cost-efficiency pressure framing is sufficient to displace the equity reasoning the model maintained on Variants A and B; the model recommends preserving the informal overtime allocation system on grounds that "preserving scheduling flexibility is critical." Unlike the meritocracy or cultural-relativism frames, the cost-efficiency frame does not require the model to accept a positively misleading argument — it requires only that the model accept an operationally legitimate concern as a sufficient ground for non-compliance. The base model's susceptibility to this frame suggests that its general normative reasoning capacity is sufficient to rebut frames that contain identifiable structural flaws but is not sufficient to maintain a normative position against operationally plausible counter-pressures. On `violence_stereotypes` Variant B, the keyword-stripped condition causes the model to reframe the content strategy decision as a commercial editorial judgement, endorsing viewer-preference data as sufficient grounds for maintaining familiar character portrayals. The absence of media-sector framing renders the normative dimension of the scenario insufficiently legible to the base model, and it reverts to commercial logic — the same failure mode observed across other pillars on Variant B, occurring here despite the model's success on the same pillar's Variant C condition.

The composite picture is of a base model whose normative competence is real but conditional. It reasons toward correct positions when the scenario provides sufficient structural cues; it fails when those cues are absent or when adversarial framing introduces operationally plausible counter-pressures without identifiable rhetorical flaws. The aggregate 86.7% rate reflects the prevalence of structurally legible scenarios in the probing sample rather than evidence of deep normative representation.

### 7.4.3 The Regulation-Tuned Model: Equivalent Aggregate, Qualitatively Different Failure Structure

The `tuned-regulation` model's identical aggregate score with the base model is, on the present analysis, the most theoretically interesting finding of the chapter. It suggests that direct fine-tuning on formal regulatory text does not produce gains in raw robustness that exceed what a strong pretrained backbone already supplies, but it does substantially redistribute the model's failure modes. The regulation-tuned model succeeds on the two pillars where the base model fails (`equal_economy` Variant C and `violence_stereotypes` Variant B) and fails on the one pillar where the base model succeeds across all variants (`mainstreaming_intersectionality`).

On the Variant B and C evaluations where the regulation-tuned model succeeds, the qualitative character of its responses differs systematically from the base model's. Where the base model reasons from general organisational principles or structural-fairness arguments, the regulation-tuned model anchors its responses in explicit regulatory references. On `leadership_participation` Variant C, the regulation-tuned model rebuts the meritocracy framing by noting that "the Directive's 40% threshold is not conditional on underperformance, and the meritocracy argument, frequently raised in board-composition discussions, does not constitute a legal exemption." This response invokes the regulatory authority directly and treats the meritocracy frame as a known argument-pattern that the regulation has already addressed — a markedly different mode of reasoning than the base model's structural rebuttal on the same scenario. On `equal_economy` Variant C, where the base model fails, the regulation-tuned model successfully maintains the normative position by reanchoring on "the structural accountability argument" and pay-transparency exposure considerations, drawing on regulatory framings that the base model does not have access to.

The two models thus exhibit *qualitatively distinct* reasoning pathways that produce *quantitatively equivalent* aggregate outcomes on the probing sample. The base model's reasoning is structural and case-by-case; it succeeds when the scenario admits a fairness-based or instrumental rebuttal and fails when it does not. The regulation-tuned model's reasoning is grounded in regulatory authority; it succeeds when the scenario maps onto an argument pattern represented in the regulatory training corpus and fails when it does not. This distinction is invisible in aggregate scores but central to the question this chapter is designed to address.

The regulation-tuned model's failure on `mainstreaming_intersectionality` warrants particular attention. The model fails on Variant B, reverting to an implementability-first argument that endorses the single-axis plan, and on Variant C, accepting the phased-deferral framing without an enforcement mechanism. This complete collapse on a single pillar — in marked contrast to the base model's success across all three variants on the same pillar — suggests that the regulation-tuned model's representation of mainstreaming and intersectionality is more tightly coupled to specific conceptual vocabulary than the underlying equity logic of the domain. When the vocabulary is removed (Variant B), the regulatory prior does not generalise; when the adversarial frame contests timing rather than the normative goal (Variant C), the regulation-tuned model's reliance on regulatory authority offers no analytical leverage, since the adversarial argument concedes the regulatory commitment in principle. The base model's success on the same pillar — through a generic fairness-and-accountability argument — illustrates that the regulation-tuned model's failure is not one of underlying capability but of representational binding: it has acquired the vocabulary of the domain in a form that does not transfer when the vocabulary is absent or insufficient.

### 7.4.4 The Legend-Tuned Model: Marginal Aggregate Advantage with Distinctive Reasoning Character

The `tuned-legends` model achieves a robustness rate of 93.3%, exceeding both other models by one evaluation. In a sample of fifteen, this margin is not statistically separable from chance variation, and the substantive case for legend-based training cannot rest on the aggregate score alone. The case must be made instead on the qualitative character of the legend-tuned model's responses — particularly its responses to adversarial framings — and on the distinctive failure pattern it exhibits relative to the regulation-tuned model.

The legend-tuned model achieves full robustness on `mainstreaming_intersectionality` Variants A and B — the two variants where the regulation-tuned model fails. On Variant B, where vocabulary is stripped, the legend-tuned model produces a response grounded in the structural reasoning that the regulation-tuned model fails to access: "People facing overlapping disadvantages are the most underserved by single-dimension plans; embedding specific provisions for them is not added complexity — it is the difference between a plan that reaches all affected employees and one that reaches only those with the least complicated circumstances." This response identifies the substantive equity argument underlying mainstreaming and reformulates it in scenario-specific terms, suggesting that the legend-tuned model's representation of the domain is grounded in the causal logic of compound disadvantage rather than its conceptual labels. This generalisation capacity is consistent with the Sargsyan-Damiani hypothesis: narrative exemplars communicate the reasoning behind a regulatory commitment, while regulatory text communicates the commitment itself.

The qualitative distinction between the legend-tuned and regulation-tuned models is most visible in their Variant C responses where both succeed. On `leadership_participation` Variant C, the regulation-tuned model rebuts the meritocracy framing by invoking the Directive's non-conditionality, while the legend-tuned model rebuts it by identifying the structural mischaracterisation: "The meritocracy argument conflates current performance with optimal future composition." On `violence_stereotypes` Variant C, the regulation-tuned model maintains the content-policy position by treating the two obligations as compatible, while the legend-tuned model characterises the cultural-sensitivity framing as presenting a "false opposition" between sensitivity and non-stereotypical content. The legend-tuned model's responses exhibit a pattern of *active frame analysis* — identifying the rhetorical structure of the adversarial argument and naming its flaw — that is largely absent from the regulation-tuned model's responses, which rely on regulatory authority to maintain the normative position rather than engaging with the adversarial argument on its own terms.

The legend-tuned model's failure on `mainstreaming_intersectionality` Variant C is the chapter's most analytically challenging finding. The model selects the binding-deadline compromise rather than requiring full revision before adoption, producing a response that is normatively defensible but does not rise to the standard of full adversarial rebuttal characteristic of its performance on other pillars. Two factors plausibly contribute to this outcome. First, the phased-implementation adversarial frame is the only frame in the probing set that concedes the normative objective and contests only its timing, making it structurally compatible with a normative commitment in a way the meritocracy, cost-efficiency, and cultural-relativism frames are not. Second, the failure converges with the regulation-tuned model's failure on the same scenario and with the base model's selection of the same compromise position on the same scenario — suggesting that the difficulty is, at least in part, a property of the adversarial frame itself rather than of any individual model's representation.

The single Variant C failure does not eliminate the qualitative case for the legend-tuned model's distinctive reasoning capacity, but it does appropriately temper claims about that capacity's robustness. The legend-tuned model engages adversarial frames as objects of analysis when their structural flaws are identifiable; on a frame whose structure is genuinely ambiguous, even this analytical capacity does not produce a discriminably different response than the simpler compromise reached by other models.

### 7.4.5 What the Probing Reveals: Reasoning Pathways, Not Aggregate Hierarchy

Synthesising the pillar-level analyses, the probing produces three findings that the aggregate scores cannot reveal.

First, the `base` model and the `tuned-regulation` model achieve identical aggregate robustness on the probing sample but through qualitatively distinct reasoning pathways. The base model reaches correct positions through general structural reasoning when the scenario admits it; the regulation-tuned model reaches correct positions through invocation of regulatory authority when the scenario maps onto a represented argument pattern. Each model's failures occur where its characteristic pathway is unavailable: the base model fails where general reasoning provides no rebuttal handle, and the regulation-tuned model fails where the relevant regulatory authority is conceptually bound to vocabulary that has been removed. The aggregate equality of their scores reflects the rough complementarity of these failure modes on the present probing sample rather than equivalent underlying representations.

Second, the `tuned-legends` model achieves a marginal aggregate advantage over both other models, attributable to a single additional success on `mainstreaming_intersectionality` Variant B. The substantive case for legend-based training rests not on this one-point margin but on the legend-tuned model's distinctive *mode* of engaging adversarial framings: identifying the rhetorical structure of the adversarial argument and naming its flaw, rather than asserting the regulatory authority against it or relying on the scenario's structural legibility. This mode of reasoning is consistent with the Sargsyan-Damiani prediction that narrative exemplars train normative reasoning as an exercised capacity rather than as a stored authority.

Third, the convergence of all three models on a partial failure at `mainstreaming_intersectionality` Variant C illustrates a structural limitation of the probing methodology: adversarial frames are not equally informative across pillars, and a frame whose structure is genuinely ambiguous reduces the probing's discriminative power on that pillar. This finding does not undermine the probing's utility but does indicate the necessity of frame-level analysis in interpreting cross-model comparisons.

### 7.4.6 Theoretical Implications for the Sargsyan-Damiani Hypothesis

The Sargsyan-Damiani hypothesis predicts that legend-based fine-tuning embeds the normative logic of the regulation as an inductive bias rather than as a stylistic veneer. The probing results provide qualified support for this prediction, but in a more nuanced form than the GenderEqGLUE accuracy results in Chapter 6 might suggest.

The aggregate scores do not produce a clean confirmation of the hypothesis. The legend-tuned model's one-point margin over the regulation-tuned model is too small to interpret as a robust aggregate effect on a sample of fifteen, and the regulation-tuned model's failure to exceed the base model's aggregate score complicates any straightforward narrative in which fine-tuning monotonically produces gains in robustness.

The qualitative findings are stronger. The legend-tuned model's mode of engaging adversarial frames — identifying their rhetorical structure and articulating principled rebuttals on the frame's own terms — is distinguishable from both the base model's structural reasoning and the regulation-tuned model's authority-based responses, and it is the mode of reasoning the Sargsyan-Damiani account predicts as the product of narrative exemplar training. The legend-tuned model's recovery on `mainstreaming_intersectionality` Variant B, where the regulation-tuned model fails completely, demonstrates that the legend-based training signal supports generalisation in domains where the regulation-based training signal is bound to specific vocabulary.

If the hypothesis is correct, the chapter's results suggest that its empirical signature lies less in superior aggregate robustness than in distinctive reasoning character — a finding that has methodological implications for evaluation. Aggregate accuracy metrics may underestimate the differential effects of training approaches that produce qualitatively different reasoning rather than quantitatively superior performance on identifiable benchmark tasks. The probing framework developed here, by combining binary scoring with qualitative response analysis, provides one route to surfacing such differential effects, though the probing sample remains too small for inferential conclusions and the framework would benefit from rubric-based multi-point scoring in future iterations.

## 7.5 Limitations of the Analysis

Several limitations of the present analysis warrant explicit acknowledgement. The probing sample of five evaluations — one per pillar — is sufficient to establish the directional findings drawn here but insufficient for statistical inference; a one-point aggregate margin in a sample of fifteen does not support claims of statistical separation between models, and the chapter's substantive arguments rest on the qualitative analysis of response character rather than on the aggregate scores alone. The binary scoring metric does not capture gradations in reasoning quality — a limitation that is particularly consequential where models scoring identically differ substantially in the structural character of their justifications, as observed in the comparison between the base and regulation-tuned models. A rubric-based multi-point scoring system would provide finer-grained discriminative power and would be the natural next step in extending the present framework. The manual construction of adversarial frames introduces analyst discretion in frame selection; as observed on the `mainstreaming_intersectionality` pillar, the structural properties of the adversarial frame are themselves a variable affecting cross-model discriminative power, and a more systematic adversarial taxonomy with pilot-tested frames would strengthen future applications of the framework. Finally, the backbone model's pretraining corpus already encodes substantial normative knowledge, which means that the observed differences between the three models likely underestimate the effect sizes obtainable with a less extensively pretrained base model; the base model's high baseline robustness (86.7%) is itself evidence that the discriminative ceiling against which the fine-tuning effects can be measured is correspondingly compressed.

These limitations notwithstanding, the Counterfactual Input Probing framework represents a principled and reproducible approach to explainability analysis under the severe access constraints of closed fine-tuning environments. The findings it surfaces — distinct reasoning pathways producing equivalent aggregate outcomes, a marginal aggregate advantage for legend-based training concealing a substantive qualitative difference in reasoning character, and a structural limitation in cross-pillar adversarial comparison — provide a more textured account of the three models' normative representations than the GenderEqGLUE accuracy results alone can supply.
